# Research Spike v0.1: Ribbed / HCB Slab Adoption in Ethiopia

## Purpose

This is a first test of whether a structured research loop can turn a field observation into useful, evidence-traceable decision support.

This is one case study, not a full research platform.

The goal is **not** to prove that Nebyu's vaulted floor system is good. The goal is to understand what can be learned from a prior floor/slab-system adoption pathway in Ethiopia.

---

## Research Trigger

During a June 2026 conversation, Robel noted that Ethiopian condominium construction appears to have shifted from more traditional poured concrete slab approaches toward ribbed / HCB slab systems, and that ribbed / HCB slab systems are now widely used.

That observation may be important because Nebyu Haile's vaulted floor system is also a floor/slab system innovation. If Ethiopia has already normalized one major floor-system transition, that adoption pathway may contain useful lessons.

---

## Core Research Question

**How did ribbed / HCB slab systems become adopted and normalized in Ethiopian condominium construction in practice, and what does that imply about how construction innovations gain approval, trust, and scale?**

---

## Why This Matters

This could affect how Nebyu and TCUS think about:

- the correct baseline for cost, weight, and carbon comparisons;
- whether Nebyu should compare against solid RC slabs, ribbed / HCB slabs, or multiple current systems;
- how construction innovations become trusted in Ethiopia;
- which actors matter in the permission chain: engineers, universities, contractors, suppliers, government housing programs, standards bodies, or others;
- what kind of first pilot or partner pathway may be realistic.

---

## What Would Make This Spike Useful

A useful output should help answer at least some of these:

- Is Robel's observation supported by available evidence?
- When and where did ribbed / HCB slabs become common?
- Was adoption driven by government housing programs, engineers, contractors, suppliers, universities, standards, cost pressure, or other factors?
- What problem did ribbed / HCB slabs solve?
- What was the prior baseline?
- What appears to be the current baseline?
- What adoption-pathway lessons might transfer to Nebyu's vaulted floor system?
- What lessons probably do not transfer?

---

## What Would Make This Spike Useless

This spike would be much less useful if it:

- only explains ribbed slabs technically but not how they were adopted;
- produces a smooth narrative without evidence;
- infers an adoption pathway without sources;
- cannot be reviewed or corrected by Robel;
- does not identify implications for Nebyu's baseline or pathway thinking;
- produces general construction history but no permission-chain insight;
- claims a complete adoption history where the available evidence is actually fragmented.

---

## Non-Expectation Clause

This spike does not assume that a complete historical adoption record exists online.

If evidence is fragmented, scattered, or insufficient, say so clearly. Do not manufacture a clean adoption story.

A partial adoption model is acceptable if it is honest about uncertainty.

---

## What "Adoption" Means Here

Do not assume "widely used" or "dominant" unless evidence supports that wording.

In this spike, adoption may mean one or more of the following:

- use in public condominium programs;
- common contractor practice;
- inclusion in standards, manuals, or design references;
- procurement normalization;
- university / engineering normalization;
- supply-chain availability;
- repeated use across multiple projects or building types.

Where possible, specify which kind of adoption is actually supported by evidence.

### Adoption Level

When claiming adoption, distinguish between:

**Implemented adoption**
- used in actual built projects.

**Institutional adoption**
- included in programs, procurement, standards, codes, or official guidance.

**Conceptual adoption**
- discussed in papers, manuals, proposals, or academic settings.

Do not treat conceptual or institutional adoption as proof of implemented adoption unless there is evidence of real built use.

## Source Strategy

Please search beyond English `.com` results.

Prioritize:

- Ethiopian government sources;
- Ethiopian university sources;
- building codes, standards, manuals, or housing program documents;
- engineering / construction references;
- contractor or supplier documents;
- credible local news or government media;
- English and Amharic / local-language sources where possible.

### Suggested Search Lanes

#### 1. English / International Sources

Use for orientation and donor / academic reports.

Example query themes:

- Ethiopia condominium ribbed slab HCB
- Ethiopia condominium hollow concrete block slab
- Ethiopia housing ribbed slab system
- Addis Ababa condominium HCB slab
- Ethiopia low-cost housing ribbed slab
- Ethiopia building code ribbed slab hollow block
- Ethiopian condominium program construction technology
- Integrated Housing Development Program ribbed slab Ethiopia

#### 2. Ethiopian Domains

Prioritize `.gov.et`, `.edu.et`, Ethiopian university sites, construction-industry sites, and local sources.

Example query themes:

- `site:.gov.et condominium ribbed slab`
- `site:.gov.et hollow concrete block slab Ethiopia`
- `site:.edu.et Ethiopia ribbed slab`
- `site:aau.edu.et hollow concrete block slab`
- `site:eiabc.edu.et condominium slab`
- `site:.et condominium HCB slab`

#### 3. Amharic / Local-Language Sources

Search local-language terms if supported by the tools.

If Amharic sources are used, include:

- original title or snippet;
- English translation;
- source link;
- confidence note;
- whether Robel should verify.

Please include the actual local-language search terms used if any.

## Evidence Strength Tiers

Please classify sources by strength.

### Tier A: Strong Evidence

Examples:

- Ethiopian government documents;
- standards / codes;
- official housing program documents;
- Ethiopian university papers, theses, or technical reports;
- official engineering manuals or design references.

### Tier B: Medium Evidence

Examples:

- donor reports;
- engineering firm documents;
- contractor or supplier documentation;
- industry publications;
- credible professional presentations.

### Tier C: Weak Signals / Leads

Examples:

- news articles;
- interviews;
- blogs;
- social media posts;
- secondary summaries;
- unsourced web pages.

Tier C sources may be useful leads, but should not carry major conclusions by themselves.

Avoid treating Reddit, Quora, generic blogs, SEO pages, or unsourced summaries as evidence. They may be used only as leads.

---

## Epistemic Status Labels

For major claims, clearly label the epistemic status:

**Fact**
- directly supported by source evidence.

**Reported**
- stated by a source or person, but not independently verified.

**Interpretation**
- a reasoned inference from available evidence.

**Unknown**
- not supported by available evidence.

Do not collapse facts, reported claims, interpretations, and unknowns into one smooth story.

---

## Adoption Mechanisms to Tag

When discussing adoption, tag the likely mechanism where possible.

Possible mechanisms:

- cost pressure;
- reduced dead load / structural efficiency;
- material availability;
- contractor practice;
- government housing program adoption;
- standards / code acceptance;
- university or engineering influence;
- supplier availability;
- labor / training pathway;
- procurement convenience;
- construction speed;
- donor / NGO influence;
- seismic or structural performance considerations.

Do not default to "it was cheaper" unless evidence supports that mechanism.

Mechanism tags are optional annotations. They should not be treated as proven unless supported by evidence.

## Execution Notes for Operator

Please run this in phases rather than trying to complete the whole spike in one pass.

### Phase 1: Source Search

Run the English, Ethiopian-domain, and local-language search lanes.

Collect:

- source links;
- titles;
- snippets;
- PDFs;
- source type;
- language;
- date, if available;
- short notes on relevance.

**Do not synthesize yet.**

### Phase 2: Evidence Table

Build the evidence table from the collected sources.

The evidence table is the primary artifact.

Use only two required classification axes:

#### Evidence Strength

- Tier A
- Tier B
- Tier C

#### Epistemic Status

- Fact
- Reported
- Interpretation
- Unknown

Adoption mechanism may be included as an optional tag.

### Phase 3: Uncertainty Register and Case Memo

Write the uncertainty register before the narrative memo.

Separate:

- what is documented;
- what is reported;
- what is interpreted;
- what remains unknown;
- what would change our mind.

Then write the short case memo.

Do not produce a single unified explanation unless multiple independent high-confidence sources converge.

If the evidence is fragmented, say so clearly.

### Phase 4: Decision Implications

Only after the evidence table, uncertainty register, and memo are complete, summarize implications for Nebyu and TCUS.

Focus on:

- baseline comparison;
- permission-chain lessons;
- partner discovery implications;
- questions for Robel;
- questions for Nebyu;
- questions requiring an Ethiopian engineer, contractor, university contact, or housing-program actor.

---

## Required Outputs

### 1. Evidence Table

Include columns for:

- claim;
- source title;
- URL;
- source type;
- evidence tier;
- epistemic status;
- language;
- relevant quote or snippet;
- reliability / confidence;
- adoption level, if applicable;
- adoption mechanism, if applicable;
- notes;
- needs Robel verification?

The evidence table is the most important output.

### 2. Uncertainty Register

Separate:

- documented facts;
- reported claims;
- plausible interpretations;
- unknowns;
- contradictory evidence;
- what would change our mind;
- items requiring Robel or local expert validation.

This should be completed before the final memo.

### 3. Short Case Memo

Answer in narrative form, but keep uncertainty visible.

Suggested sections:

- What appears to have happened
- What is documented
- What is reported but not fully verified
- How adoption may have occurred
- Which actors may have mattered
- What evidence supports the working model
- What remains uncertain

The case memo is secondary to the evidence table and uncertainty register.

### 4. Decision Implications for Nebyu / TCUS

Include:

- implications for Nebyu's comparison baseline;
- whether solid RC slab, ribbed / HCB slab, or multiple baselines should be considered;
- implications for partner discovery;
- possible permission-chain lessons;
- questions to ask Nebyu next;
- questions to ask Robel next;
- questions requiring an Ethiopian engineer, university contact, contractor, or government/housing-program actor.

---

## Required Claim Discipline

For major claims, clearly separate:

- Documented facts
- Reported claims
- Interpretations
- Unknowns
- Items needing local verification

Do not infer adoption pathways without evidence.

Do not collapse documented facts, reported claims, and interpretations into one smooth story.

Do not produce a single unified explanation unless multiple independent high-confidence sources converge.

---

## Final Guardrail

A good result may be incomplete.

It is acceptable for the final answer to say:

> Available sources do not support a complete adoption history. The evidence suggests several possible mechanisms, but key parts of the pathway remain uncertain.

That is better than a polished but unsupported narrative.

The goal is not certainty. The goal is to reduce uncertainty enough to help Nebyu and TCUS think more clearly about baseline comparison, permission chains, and adoption pathway options.
