# Research Spike v0.1: Ribbed / HCB Slab Adoption in Ethiopia

**Purpose:** This document synthesizes research on how ribbed / Hollow Concrete Block (HCB) slab systems became adopted in Ethiopian condominium construction, serving as decision support for Nebyu Haile's vaulted floor system and The Chamber of Us (TCUS).

**Search Strategy:** Utilized English queries, Ethiopian academic/government domains (`site:.edu.et`, `site:.gov.et`), and translated Amharic terms (ሪብድ ስላብ for ribbed slab, ብሎኬት for concrete block). Evidence primarily surfaced in Addis Ababa University (AAU) engineering theses, government-affiliated housing manuals, and donor (GIZ/GTZ) reports.

## 1. Executive Summary: Short Case Memo

**What appears to have happened:**
The normalization of ribbed / HCB slab systems in Ethiopia was not a slow, organic evolution driven by individual contractors optimizing for cost. Instead, it was a massive, top-down institutional shift initiated by the Ethiopian government's Integrated Housing Development Program (IHDP) starting around 2004, designed in collaboration with international donors like GTZ.

**How adoption may have occurred:**
Faced with a massive housing deficit, the government realized that conventional cast-in-situ concrete slabs were too slow (due to 14-day formwork requirements) and too reliant on imported steel and cement. They adopted the composite precast beam and HCB slab because it allowed the manufacturing process to be decentralized. The government subsidized the creation of Micro and Small Enterprises (MSEs), providing them with land and training to manufacture HCBs and precast beams. This aligned the engineering solution (lighter slabs, less formwork) with a macroeconomic imperative (mass job creation).

**Which actors may have mattered:**
The permission chain was highly centralized. The Ministry of Urban Development and Construction (MUDC) mandated the standard typologies. Universities (like AAU) provided the ongoing structural validation and testing. The actual manufacturing was pushed to thousands of MSEs, while contractors were essentially handed a pre-designed, pre-sourced system to assemble.

## 3. Uncertainty Register & Unproven Claims

**What is Documented (Facts):**
* The baseline prior to the mid-2000s was conventional cast-in-situ reinforced concrete (RC) flat slabs, requiring extensive imported cement, rebar, and 14-day timber formwork cycles.
* The widespread adoption of ribbed/HCB slabs was triggered by the Integrated Housing Development Program (IHDP) (20/80 or 40/60 condominiums) launched around 2004/2005.
* The IHDP mandated a standardized structural system to decentralize manufacturing to Micro and Small Enterprises (MSEs) for mass employment.

**Claims That Are Still Not Proven:**
* **"Ribbed / HCB slabs are dominant across *all* Ethiopian construction"**
  * *Status:* Unknown / Unproven. Evidence confirms dominance in government-subsidized programs. It remains uncertain if private/commercial sectors adopted this as their baseline organically.
* **"Adoption was a purely organic, market-driven contractor choice"**
  * *Status:* Inferred False. Adoption was heavily subsidized top-down via government mandates linking housing design to MSE job creation targets.
* **"The system is structurally trusted by engineers without reservations"**
  * *Status:* Uncertain. The literature frequently notes high defect rates, deflections, and poor workmanship associated with MSE-produced blocks.

## 4. Decision Implications for Nebyu & TCUS

**Implications for Baseline Comparison:**
* **The Baseline is HCB, not Solid RC:** Comparing Nebyu's vaulted floor system exclusively against solid cast-in-situ concrete slabs may be a strawman argument in the Ethiopian context. The true competitor for affordable/mass housing is the **28cm thick precast beam + HCB ribbed slab**. Nebyu’s calculations for weight, carbon, and cost must beat or match *this* composite system.
* **Formwork is the Enemy:** The literature explicitly cites the reduction of timber formwork as a primary reason for moving away from flat slabs. If Nebyu’s vaulted system requires complex or slow-curing formwork, it will struggle against the HCB system.

**Permission-Chain and Partner Discovery Lessons:**
* **The MSE Pathway:** The mass-housing supply chain is built around MSEs. If Nebyu’s earthen blocks or Compressed Stabilized Earth Blocks (CSEB) can be manufactured locally by low-skill MSEs using simple presses, this provides a compelling socio-economic argument for adoption.
* **University Validation:** The sheer volume of AAU theses testing HCB spans indicates local academic validation is standard. Sponsoring a pilot test with an Ethiopian university engineering department is highly recommended.

**Questions Requiring Local Verification (For Robel / Field Contacts):**
1. Are ribbed / HCB slabs mainly used in government 20/80 and 40/60 condominiums, or do private developers also demand them now?
2. Do local contractors actually *like* the HCB ribbed slabs, or do they use them due to government mandates/supply chain convenience?
3. If testing a new earthen vault block, would approval be needed from the Ministry of Urban Development, or could a private engineer stamp it for a pilot?
