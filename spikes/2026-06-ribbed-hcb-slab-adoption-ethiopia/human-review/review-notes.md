# Human Review Notes

Date: June 21, 2026  
Reviewer: Kevin Schmidt

I am not using LLMs for this section. These are my first human impressions after reviewing the model generations, cross-model comparison, cleaned findings, and claims-needing-verification register.

My first impression is that this was a great use of AI. I like the idea of triangulating toward some sort of truth rather than treating any single model output as the answer.

I think what ChatGPT did with the initial README structure and prompting was incredibly smart. It created a better research container before the actual model generations happened. That matters because the repo is not just collecting outputs. It is shaping a process for uncertainty reduction.

It was especially interesting to see that Claude surfaced findings around cost savings and baseline fairness. Multiple models seemed to notice that the relative baseline being a flat reinforced-concrete slab is something worth talking about. That feels important for Nebyu’s vaulted floor work because the fair comparison may depend on the actual incumbent system in the target building type.

This spike also surfaced good-enough leads that we can take into Slack or direct messages and ask better follow-up questions. Robel’s anchoring remains important here. The model outputs are not the ultimate reality, but they help us identify what to ask people who are closer to the ground.

This work also elevated TCUS structurally. Pathfinder Research is becoming more than a folder of AI outputs. It is becoming a repeatable research process: prompt carefully, generate independently, compare across models, preserve uncertainty, identify verification needs, and then use human/local review to move closer to reality.

This spike helped contribute to the current v0.3 working-paper direction. The main value was not that the models “solved” the ribbed/HCB adoption history. They did not. The value was that they exposed useful tensions:

- ribbed/HCB seems to have been real and important in Ethiopian housing practice
- the exact adoption pathway is still not fully documented
- public housing, procurement, MSE production, standards, and engineering familiarity may all have mattered
- flat RC may not always be the right comparison baseline
- local verification is necessary before making confident claims

My takeaway is that this spike should be treated as a strong research lead, not a final answer. The next useful step is to bring the claims-needing-verification register into conversations with Robel, Nebyu, Ethiopian engineers, contractors, or quantity surveyors, and use their feedback to understand the actual practice more clearly.
