# Research Spike v0.3 Prompt: Permission Chain and Adoption Pathway of Ribbed / HCB Slab Systems in Ethiopia

## Status

Draft prompt for multi-model / human research execution.

This is a follow-up to the first ribbed / HCB slab adoption spike.

The first spike asked whether ribbed / HCB slabs appear to have become normalized in Ethiopian condominium or building practice.

This spike asks a different question:

> What evidence exists for each stage of normalization, who was involved, and how did a structural floor system become acceptable and ordinary in Ethiopian construction practice?

Do not treat this as a broad memo assignment. Treat it as a structured evidence-gathering and causal-inference task.

---

# 1. Purpose

The purpose of this spike is to reconstruct, as carefully as evidence allows, the permission chain and adoption pathway for ribbed / hollow concrete block (HCB) slab systems in Ethiopia.

The goal is not to prove that ribbed / HCB slabs were adopted through a clean, linear pathway.

The goal is to identify what can actually be supported by evidence, what remains unknown, and what lessons may or may not transfer to Nebyu Haile’s vaulted floor system.

---

# 2. Core Research Question

How did ribbed / HCB slab systems move from being a possible structural floor system to being treated as ordinary or acceptable practice in Ethiopian condominium and broader building construction?

---

# 3. Key Definitions

## Adoption

First documented real-world use of ribbed / HCB slab systems in Ethiopian buildings.

Adoption evidence may include:

- project drawings;
- bills of quantities;
- tender documents;
- construction reports;
- photographs;
- case-study buildings;
- contractor records;
- university theses documenting real projects.

## Normalization

A later and stronger condition where engineers, universities, suppliers, procurement systems, contractors, or code/approval bodies treat the system as ordinary rather than experimental.

Normalization evidence may include:

- code provisions;
- standard drawings;
- routine university design templates;
- repeated project use;
- supplier availability;
- procurement specifications;
- MSE training manuals;
- contractor practice documents;
- multiple independent project records.

## Baseline Status

The point at which ribbed / HCB slabs are not merely used, but become a realistic comparison baseline for evaluating newer floor-system innovations.

Baseline status must not be assumed from one or two descriptive sources.

---

# 4. Required Epistemic Rules

## 4.1 No Narrative-First Writing

Do not begin by telling a clean adoption story.

Begin with evidence.

Only synthesize a pathway after evidence has been organized by time period, actor, decision point, and strength.

## 4.2 No False Completion

If no evidence exists for a field, write:

> No evidence found in this phase.

Do not infer, interpolate, or fill empty table cells with plausible guesses.

## 4.3 Thesis Language Is Not Enough

Descriptive thesis language such as “common,” “frequently built,” or “widely used” is not sufficient by itself to prove adoption, normalization, or dominance.

Such language may be used only as a signal, unless corroborated by at least one of the following:

- project drawings;
- BOQ / tender documents;
- procurement records;
- code provisions;
- contractor documents;
- supplier records;
- multiple independent theses from different years or institutions.

## 4.4 Distinguish Direct Evidence From Inference

Every claim must be marked as:

- Directly supported;
- Partially supported;
- Inferred;
- Unsupported;
- Contradicted;
- No evidence found in this phase.

## 4.5 Negative Evidence Is Valuable

Evidence that weakens the leading explanation is not a failure. It is one of the main outputs of the spike.

---

# 5. Kill Criteria

The working assumption that ribbed / HCB slabs became normalized through a distinct Ethiopian adoption pathway should be reconsidered if evidence shows that:

1. Ribbed slabs were already present in Ethiopian codes before major condominium programs.
2. Ribbed slabs were inherited directly from common international reinforced-concrete design practice with no meaningful local transition.
3. Condominium programs primarily used other slab systems.
4. Ribbed / HCB systems never exceeded niche usage despite appearing frequently in academic literature.
5. Evidence remains too fragmented to reconstruct a meaningful pathway.
6. Ribbed / HCB became common only in academic discussion, not in documented project delivery.
7. The same time period shows strong use of competing slab systems without evidence that ribbed / HCB became preferred.

If any kill criterion appears plausible, stop and document it clearly before continuing synthesis.

---

# 6. Required Time Slicing

Organize evidence into the following time windows.

Do not collapse the timeline into vague “early,” “mid,” or “recent” periods.

| Time Window | Purpose |
|---|---|
| 1990–2000 | Pre-IHDP baseline and inherited engineering practice |
| 2000–2005 | Early transition / pre-scale-up / early IHDP planning |
| 2005–2010 | IHDP scale-up and construction-sector mobilization |
| 2010–2015 | Possible normalization phase |
| 2015–present | Stabilized baseline, private-sector uptake, or competing systems |

For each source, identify which time window it supports.

If a source was published later but describes earlier practice, distinguish publication date from event date.

# 7. Workstream Priority Weights

Do not treat all workstreams as equally important.

Use the following approximate priority weights when allocating research effort:

| Workstream | Priority Weight | Rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| A. Government / IHDP / procurement pathway | 30% | Highest chance of finding adoption/standardization evidence |
| B. Code and standards history | 15% | Clarifies whether practice followed code or code reflected practice |
| C. University and thesis normalization | 25% | Strong evidence of engineering discourse, but interpretive |
| D. Contractor and supplier ecosystem | 15% | Important for implementation, but noisy |
| E. Baseline shift analysis | 5% | Derived from other streams, not primary evidence |
| F. Innovation approval pathway reconstruction | 10% | Synthesis layer, not a source category |

These weights are guidance, not rigid quotas.

---

# 8. Contradiction-First Pass

Before proposing any adoption pathway model, search explicitly for evidence that contradicts a linear success narrative.

Record at least one contradiction, or explicitly state:

> No contradiction found in this phase.

Look for:

- cases where ribbed / HCB was not used in similar buildings;
- competing slab systems used in the same period;
- documented failures, deflections, quality issues, or complaints;
- switching away from ribbed / HCB systems;
- studies showing solid slabs were cheaper under some assumptions;
- code or design constraints limiting use;
- regional differences between Addis Ababa and other Ethiopian cities.

Output this as a Negative Evidence Register.

# 20. Output Discipline

The final answer should not be a polished story unless the evidence supports one.

Prefer this structure:

1. What is directly supported.
2. What is partially supported.
3. What is contradicted or complicated.
4. What remains unknown.
5. What evidence would change confidence.
6. What this implies for Nebyu.

Use cautious language:

- “The evidence supports…”
- “The evidence suggests…”
- “This remains unproven…”
- “No evidence found in this phase…”
- “This claim should be treated as a lead, not a finding…”

Avoid:

- “This proves…”
- “Clearly…”
- “Widely adopted…”
- “Dominant baseline…”
- “Government mandated…”
- “The adoption pathway was…”

unless directly supported by strong evidence.

---

# 21. Final One-Sentence Mission

Reconstruct the evidence-supported pathway by which ribbed / HCB slabs became acceptable or ordinary in Ethiopian construction practice, while making uncertainty, contradiction, missing documents, and Nebyu transferability explicit.
