## Purpose

This file tracks claims that are plausible, important, or strategically useful, but not yet strong enough to treat as accepted findings.

Use this file as a work queue for source cleanup, local engineering review, and partner conversations.

## Verification Labels

Use these labels as claims move through review:

- **Unverified lead**: surfaced by one or more model generations, but not yet checked.
- **Reported claim**: found in a company, media, donor, or promotional source.
- **Documented fact**: supported by a source that directly documents the claim.
- **Technical inference**: reasonable interpretation based on sources, but not directly stated.
- **Needs local review**: requires Ethiopian engineer, regulator, contractor, or project actor input.
- **Rejected / not supported**: should not be repeated unless new evidence appears.

## Priority 1: Kubik

### Claim K1: Kubik products are 40% cheaper than conventional cement-based construction

- **Current status:** Reported claim.
- **Why it matters:** This is central to Kubik’s value proposition and to any comparison with HCB, CSEB, or conventional masonry.
- **Current concern:** The claim may come from company or media interviews rather than independent BOQ comparison.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - project-level BOQ
  - independent cost study
  - unit cost comparison against HCB, fired brick, concrete block, or conventional walling
  - clear definition of what is being compared: wall only, shell, complete building, or per-square-meter construction
- **Do not say yet:** “Kubik is 40% cheaper.”
- **Safer wording:** “Kubik reports lower cost than conventional cement-based alternatives, but the exact comparison needs independent verification.”

### Claim K2: Kubik products are structurally approved for Ethiopian multi-story housing

- **Current status:** Unverified lead.
- **Why it matters:** Structural use class changes the adoption pathway and risk profile.
- **Current concern:** The public evidence appears stronger for walls, partitions, components, and named projects than for broad structural approval in multi-story residential buildings.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - Ethiopian regulatory approval
  - engineering signoff
  - technical data sheets
  - structural test reports
  - fire and seismic compliance documentation
  - project drawings showing use class
- **Do not say yet:** “Kubik can replace conventional structural walls in multi-story housing.”
- **Safer wording:** “Kubik has visible built-project traction, but its public structural approval status and use class need verification.”

### Claim K3: Kubik has independent fire, toxicity, seismic, and long-term durability testing

- **Current status:** Reported claim / needs source cleanup.
- **Why it matters:** Without these, partner-facing claims will sound like marketing.
- **Current concern:** Some sources imply testing or technical documents, but public reports may not be available.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - independent lab reports
  - Intertek or equivalent reports, if claimed
  - Ethiopian Standards Institute / ESA documentation
  - fire rating
  - toxicity / VOC / smoke testing
  - UV aging
  - creep and long-term load testing
  - connection and shear testing
- **Safer wording:** “Kubik appears to have technical testing claims, but the underlying reports should be requested before relying on them.”

### Claim K4: Kubik has reached commercial adoption in Ethiopia

- **Current status:** Partially supported.
- **Why it matters:** Commercial product is different from prototype.
- **Current concern:** “Commercial adoption” can mean factory-backed product and named customers, but not necessarily routine market use.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - list of Ethiopian projects
  - installed quantities
  - paying customers
  - repeat purchases
  - occupancy or use status
  - customer type: NGO, private developer, public agency, industrial buyer
- **Safer wording:** “Kubik appears to have moved beyond concept into factory-backed production and named projects, but routine market adoption is not yet proven.”

## Priority 2: Agrostone

### Claim A1: Agrostone reduced wall construction cost by 37% to 50%

- **Current status:** Reported / technical claim.
- **Why it matters:** This is the core Agrostone value proposition.
- **Current concern:** The cost claim may be context-specific, dated, or based on selected comparisons.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - source page and exact quote
  - comparison basis
  - year
  - whether plaster/paint/labor/transport are included
  - whether claim still applies today
- **Safer wording:** “Some sources report substantial wall-cost reductions for Agrostone, but the basis and current validity need source cleanup.”

### Claim A2: Agrostone was used widely in Addis Ababa housing projects

- **Current status:** Partially supported.
- **Why it matters:** Determines whether Agrostone is a real adoption case or just a pilot.
- **Current concern:** “Used in housing projects” is weaker than “widely used” or “standardized across IHDP.”
- **Evidence needed:**
  - project names
  - number of housing units
  - standard drawings/specifications
  - procurement documents
  - official AAHDPO / housing bureau documents
- **Safer wording:** “Agrostone reached real housing-project use, but the scale and duration of that use need verification.”

### Claim A3: Agrostone was mainly internal partition / non-load-bearing use

- **Current status:** Likely but needs verification.
- **Why it matters:** Use class affects how comparable Agrostone is to Kubik, HCB, and Nebyu’s floor system.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - technical specification
  - standard details
  - source quote describing internal/external use
  - current practice confirmation from Ethiopian architect/engineer
- **Safer wording:** “Agrostone appears to have been used mainly as a wall or partition panel, but exact use class needs cleanup.”

### Claim A4: Moisture resistance limited Agrostone’s adoption

- **Current status:** Partially supported.
- **Why it matters:** This makes Agrostone both an adoption case and a cautionary tale.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - technical studies on water absorption / moisture degradation
  - field reports of failures
  - distinction between lab material limitation and actual project failure
  - current mitigation practices
- **Safer wording:** “Moisture resistance appears to be a documented concern, but its effect on real-world adoption needs verification.”

## Priority 3: CSEB / Hydraform / Interlocking Earth Blocks

### Claim C1: CSEB is cheaper than HCB in Ethiopian practice

- **Current status:** Unverified / reported claim.
- **Why it matters:** Cost is central to any adoption pathway.
- **Current concern:** Cost advantages may depend heavily on soil, stabilizer, labor, transport, scale, and wall type.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - Ethiopian BOQ or thesis comparison
  - block production cost
  - stabilizer percentage
  - labor productivity
  - transport assumptions
  - durability and maintenance assumptions
- **Safer wording:** “CSEB is often presented as lower-cost, but Ethiopian project-level cost advantage remains to be verified.”

### Claim C2: CSEB is technically suitable for Ethiopian soils in multiple regions

- **Current status:** Partially supported.
- **Why it matters:** Suitability affects whether CSEB can scale locally.
- **Current concern:** Soil suitability is site-specific.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - Ethiopian soil studies
  - region-by-region test results
  - compressive strength and water absorption
  - stabilizer requirements
  - failed soil cases as well as successful ones
- **Safer wording:** “Some Ethiopian studies suggest suitable soils exist, but CSEB requires site-specific testing and quality control.”

### Claim C3: CSEB is blocked mainly by stigma rather than technical issues

- **Current status:** Technical / social inference.
- **Why it matters:** Determines what intervention is needed: marketing, engineering, standards, or training.
- **Current concern:** Barriers may include stigma, but also moisture, seismic design, QA, contractor familiarity, and finance.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - interviews with Ethiopian engineers/developers
  - thesis or survey evidence
  - project adoption history
  - local reviewer input
- **Safer wording:** “CSEB adoption appears to face both technical and perception barriers.”

### Claim C4: Hydraform or interlocking CSEB has mainstream Ethiopian adoption

- **Current status:** Not supported yet.
- **Why it matters:** Avoids overstating a research/tooling pathway.
- **Evidence needed:**
  - named projects
  - repeated use
  - procurement records
  - institutional programs
  - current suppliers and sales volumes
- **Safer wording:** “Hydraform/CSEB has Ethiopian research and tooling activity, but mainstream adoption was not proven in this phase.”

## Human Review Questions

### Questions for Robel or another Ethiopian practicing engineer

- Which of these systems are actually visible in Ethiopian construction practice?
- Are Kubik blocks treated locally as structural, partition, or experimental?
- Is Agrostone still used, or was it mainly an IHDP-era partition material?
- Are CSEB / Hydraform systems locally trusted, stigmatized, or ignored?
- Are light-steel/prefab systems viewed as serious housing solutions or premium/import-dependent options?
- Are 3D printing and container housing viewed as real pathways or political demonstrations?
- What would Ethiopian engineers expect Nebyu’s system to be compared against?

### Questions for Nebyu / EiABC / technical reviewers

- What is the minimum full-scale prototype needed to create trust?
- Which EBCS provisions are most relevant?
- Would the first pilot be better framed as a floor-system test, a school/community building, or a small non-residential structure?
- What baseline matters most: flat slab, ribbed/HCB, or both?
- What technical package would a reviewer need before approving a bounded pilot?
