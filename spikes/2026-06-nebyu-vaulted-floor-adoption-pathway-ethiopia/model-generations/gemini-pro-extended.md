# **Innovation Adoption Pathway for Unreinforced Earthen Vaulted Floor Systems in Ethiopia**

The proposition of introducing an unreinforced earthen barrel-vaulted floor system into the Ethiopian construction sector represents a multifaceted institutional and technical challenge. Developed by Nebyu Samuel Haile as a direct alternative to conventional reinforced concrete (RC) flat slabs—which remain heavily dependent on imported, carbon-intensive cement and steel—this system proposes a fundamental shift in construction geometry, material substitution, and structural behavior[1]. By utilizing Compressed Stabilized Earth Bricks (CSEBs) in a compression-only vaulted geometry, the system theoretically achieves a 74% reduction in embodied carbon and a 62% reduction in cost compared to typical RC slabs[4].  
However, as historical analyses of construction-system innovations consistently demonstrate, technical merit and macroeconomic alignment are insufficient to guarantee market adoption. The construction industry is inherently risk-averse, bound by rigid liability frameworks, standardized design codes, and established supply chains. A novel floor system creates profound institutional questions that must be answered before widespread replication can occur: Who assumes the liability for structural failure in a highly indeterminate system? How does a municipality permit a structural typology that falls outside the standard tabular design codes of the Ethiopian Building Code Standards (EBCS)? How do local contractors accurately price and execute unfamiliar geometries without the continuous supervision of foreign experts or PhD researchers?  
This report provides an exhaustive innovation-adoption and institutional-pathway analysis. It identifies the critical trust-building mechanisms, host institutions, coalition configurations, and evidence-generating milestones required for this vaulted earthen floor system to progress from academic research into credible, institutionally supported practice in Ethiopia.

## **Phase 1 — Current Position on the Innovation Journey**

To plot a trajectory for institutional adoption, the current developmental maturity of the system must be rigorously established across three distinct dimensions: technical maturity, institutional maturity, and implementation maturity.  
Currently, the primary evidence supporting Nebyu’s system exists firmly within academic bounds. A 3-meter span physical prototype utilizing 3% and 7% cement-stabilized earth bricks (exceeding 5 MPa compressive strength) was constructed and subjected to symmetrical, asymmetrical, and point loading[4]. The prototype successfully achieved the target design live load of 2 kN/m²[4]. Furthermore, 2D equilibrium analyses have validated the system's theoretical capacity, and comparative life cycle assessments (LCA) demonstrate significant reductions in cost and carbon footprints[4].  
Despite these academic successes, the system occupies a highly fragile position on the innovation continuum. It lacks third-party field validation under uncontrolled conditions, regulatory approval from Ethiopian municipal authorities, and any history of execution by independent commercial contractors.

### **Assessment of System Maturity Dimensions**

The technical maturity of the system is moderate, situated at the prototype level. The structural mechanics under static loads are validated in a controlled environment, confirming that the unreinforced shallow earthen shell vault keeps working stresses well below the compressive strength of standard CSEBs[4]. However, long-term durability, seismic performance in highly active areas like Addis Ababa (Seismic Zone 4), and construction tolerances in uncontrolled field conditions remain entirely untested[5].  
The institutional maturity is currently low. The system lacks formal recognition under the Ethiopian Building Code Standards, specifically the recently updated EBCS EN 1996-1-1:2014 regarding the design of masonry structures[7]. No municipal building permit has been issued for this specific floor typology in an occupied multi-story urban building, meaning any immediate attempt to build it commercially would face extreme regulatory friction[9].  
The implementation maturity is similarly low. The prototype construction was executed by the primary researcher, requiring specialized knowledge to erect the temporary guides and execute the masonry with an acceptable margin of error[1]. There is no documented evidence of a local, independent Grade 1-5 contractor successfully interpreting the architectural drawings, sourcing the requisite high-quality CSEBs, and building the system without direct academic intervention.

Construction systems rarely spread through top-down mandates or because a new material is mathematically superior. They typically advance through iterative loops of demonstration, trusted institutional hosting, and subsequent contractor familiarization.

### **Key Institutional Insights from Analogues**

The historical record suggests that Nebyu's system sits uncomfortably between two proven models of adoption. The first is the "Dieste Method," where the innovator succeeds by becoming a contractor and absorbing all execution and liability risk directly[29]. The second is the "Nubian Vault Method," which succeeds by making the system so rudimentary, inexpensive, and formwork-free that illiterate farmers can adopt it without requiring formal engineering review or municipal permits[18]. Nebyu’s system proposes a highly engineered, precision-dependent geometry targeting formal, multi-story urban applications. This requires engineering sign-off, moving it away from the Nubian model, but Nebyu is a researcher, not a vertically integrated contractor like Dieste.  
The most critical analogue is the Sustainable Urban Dwelling Unit (SUDU) project in Ethiopia. Hosted by the Ethiopian Institute of Architecture, Building Construction and City Development (EiABC), SUDU proved that a beautiful, structurally sound earthen vault could be built in Addis Ababa[12]. However, post-project analyses revealed a fatal flaw in scalability: the Catalan (timbrel) vaulting technique required a level of artisan precision that local laborers could not replicate without the continuous, heavy supervision of foreign experts[16]. Furthermore, specific soils had to be imported to the site, eroding the cost benefits[16]. If Nebyu's system requires complex falsework, highly precise mortar joints, or specialized soil that cannot be found universally, it will face the exact same adoption barrier that relegated SUDU to a singular campus experiment rather than a national housing solution.

## **Phase 3 — Trust-Carriers**

In the Ethiopian construction ecosystem, an innovation cannot leap directly from a researcher's thesis to a municipal building permit. It must pass through institutions and individuals capable of absorbing, mitigating, or transferring risk. The regulatory environment in Ethiopia is strict for urban construction, governed by the Ethiopian Building Proclamation No. 624/2009 and the EBCS codes[9].  
To navigate this, actors must be categorized by their functional utility to the innovation:

* **Reviewers:** Actors who evaluate the technical merit against established codes (e.g., Ethiopian Standards Agency, municipal permit officers).  
* **Hosts:** Actors who can provide land and institutional cover for early, un-permitted experiments (e.g., EiABC, TVET colleges, international NGOs).  
* **Champions:** Actors who spend their own political or professional capital to advocate for the system, transferring their inherent trust to the new technology (e.g., senior structural engineers, academic deans).  
* **Implementers:** Actors who execute the work physically and repeatedly (e.g., Grade 1-5 contractors, trained masonry artisans).

## **Phase 4 — Building Types as Adoption Vehicles**

The choice of the *first* fully realized building is the most critical strategic decision in the adoption sequence. Buildings must not be evaluated primarily on their architectural grandeur or theoretical risk, but on their capacity to generate specific types of learning, institutional credibility, and future commercial opportunities.  
If the first building is too small (e.g., a test bay), it generates no institutional momentum and is dismissed by the industry as a laboratory curiosity. If the first building is too critical (e.g., an occupied multistory residence), the liability is too high for any municipal official to permit it or any engineer to stamp it[9].

### **Recommendations for Building Sequence**

**Most Plausible First Building:** A single-story Auxiliary Structure (such as a workshop, machinery shed, or storage building) hosted on a university campus (like EiABC) or a TVET (Technical and Vocational Education and Training) college. In this context, a vaulted roof serves functionally as a ceiling, but structurally mimics the exact behavior of a floor. Because it is not a primary occupied dwelling, life-safety liability is vastly reduced, easing the path for internal institutional approval while bypassing the Addis Ababa City Administration's rigid permitting bureaucracy.  
**Most Strategic Second Building:** A Rural Community Building or an NGO-funded rural school classroom. Rural environments in Ethiopia generally allow for more regulatory flexibility and rely heavily on local materials[48]. If Nebyu's system can be executed successfully by a rural contractor using locally pressed CSEBs, it decisively proves that the construction method is robust and not hopelessly dependent on PhD-level supervision or imported materials.  
**Building Types that Generate Excitement but Little Progress:** The "Test Pavilion." While aesthetically pleasing and structurally informative, pavilions bypass the actual friction points of real construction: installing doors, sealing windows, waterproofing, routing electrical conduits, and managing acoustic separation. They do not convince pragmatic contractors or risk-averse municipalities.  
**Institutionally Premature:** Multi-story urban housing. Until a recognized local structural engineering firm is willing to stamp the drawings, attempting a multi-story urban building will hit an immediate, insurmountable regulatory wall at the building permit office[9].

### **Identifying the Minimum Viable and Ideal Coalitions**

**Minimum Viable Coalition (for a first institutionally credible project):**  
To break ground on a meaningful structure, Nebyu must assemble a triad of actors who operate outside pure commercial incentives:

1. *The Innovator* (Nebyu) providing technical oversight, geometry control, and quality assurance.  
2. *The Institutional Host* (EiABC or a regional TVET college) providing the land, student labor, and a regulatory "sandbox" that shields the project from municipal permitting[48].  
3. *The Grant Funder* (e.g., ETH Sustainability, UN-Habitat, or an embassy innovation fund) providing $15,000–$30,000 in risk-free capital that does not demand a commercial return on investment[53].

**Ideal Coalition (for transitioning to market adoption):**  
Once the system is proven in a sandbox, transitioning to the real market requires a more complex, commercially viable coalition:

1. *The Innovator* (Nebyu).  
2. *A Progressive Local Structural Engineering Firm* willing to collaboratively translate the 2D equilibrium models into EBCS-compliant documentation, taking on the role of the technical champion.  
3. *A Government Partner* (e.g., Ministry of Urban Development) observing the project to draft future CES (Compulsory Ethiopian Standards) addendums, giving the system legal permanence[47].  
4. *A Trained Artisan Crew* acting as specialized sub-contractors. Similar to how the Nubian Vault masons operate, these crews would travel site-to-site, guaranteeing rapid execution for general contractors[21].

Evidence in the construction sector is rarely objective; it is highly contextual and deeply psychological. A load-displacement graph matters to an academic, but it is entirely useless to a general contractor trying to bid a job under a tight deadline. Evidence must be classified by its specific influence on different actors' future decisions.

**Synthesis of Evidence Impact:**

* *Evidence that reduces uncertainty:* Load tests, material strength tests showing that standard Ethiopian soil stabilized with only 6% cement can hit the necessary \>2 MPa threshold[4]. (Epistemic status: Documented fact)  
* *Evidence that creates advocates:* Post-occupancy thermal comfort data, and dramatic cost reduction audits (e.g., validating the theoretical 62% cost savings)[4]. (Epistemic status: Technical inference)  
* *Evidence that creates replication:* A highly visual, step-by-step construction manual detailing the temporary guide systems, mortar mixing, and block laying sequences. This specific evidence solves the exact problem that killed the SUDU Catalan vault's scalability—it demystifies the craft[16]. (Epistemic status: Institutional inference)

Alignment of incentives is the engine of adoption. If a system solves a problem that an institution is actively agonizing over, adoption accelerates organically. If it creates new problems, it stalls.

Because the engineer is the gatekeeper to the formal market, adoption pathways must circumvent the standard commercial engineering pipeline in the early stages, relying instead on institutional hosts who self-insure.

## **Phase 9 — Progression Pathways**

There is no single roadmap for construction innovation. Below are the most plausible sequence pathways for the system to move from its current TRL 4/5 state into institutional reality, ranked by probability of success.

### **1. The University-Led Pathway (The "SUDU 2.0" Model)**

* **Sequence:** Academic Research → University Host (EiABC) → Permanent Campus Auxiliary Building → Monitored Data → Broader Institutional Interest.  
* **Strengths:** Completely bypasses municipal permitting (universities often self-permit or have fast-track mechanisms for campus facilities); leverages free or cheap student labor; creates a highly visible demonstration for visiting policymakers.[13]  
* **Weaknesses:** Projects built on academic campuses are often dismissed by commercial contractors as "academic experiments" that cannot survive the brutal economics of the real market.[17]  
* **Key Dependency:** EiABC leadership's willingness to host another earthen vault project after the logistical challenges and eventual fade of the SUDU project.  
* **Probability:** **High.** This is the path of least institutional resistance and aligns with existing precedents.  
* **Earliest Major Milestone:** Breaking ground on a functional campus workshop.

### **2. The NGO/Donor-Led Pathway (The "Nubian Vault" Model)**

* **Sequence:** Grant Funding → Rural Demonstration (Clinic/School) → NGO Network Evaluation → Replicated Rural Rollout.  
* **Strengths:** Capital is readily available for sustainable, local-material projects (e.g., via UNIDO, AFCIA, or European embassies)[22]; rural locations have significantly lower regulatory hurdles and building code enforcement.  
* **Weaknesses:** Isolates the technology in rural or informal sectors, potentially branding it permanently as "poor man's housing." This stigma actively hinders urban adoption, which is a known socio-cultural issue in Ethiopia regarding earthen materials[50].  
* **Key Dependency:** Securing a grant explicitly focused on *low-carbon construction material innovation*.  
* **Probability:** **Medium-High.** Aligns perfectly with global climate-finance goals and rural development mandates.

### **3. The Engineer/Contractor-Led Pathway (The "Dieste" Model)**

* **Sequence:** Engineer Review → Site-Specific Commercial Project → Profit Verification → Repeat Projects.  
* **Strengths:** Directly attacks the commercial market; proves financial viability immediately; attracts private capital.  
* **Weaknesses:** Requires finding a contractor willing to take a massive financial risk on an unproven execution method, and an engineer willing to absorb the liability of an un-coded system[10].  
* **Key Dependency:** Finding a visionary, highly risk-tolerant local developer and engineer pair.  
* **Probability:** **Low.** The Ethiopian commercial construction market is currently optimized for rapid, standardized concrete and HCB execution[6]. Deviating from this means losing money.

### **4. The Standards-Led Pathway (The "LC[3]" Model)**

* **Sequence:** Lobbying Standards Agency → Pilot inclusion in EBCS → Government Procurement → Market Adoption.  
* **Strengths:** Permanently institutionalizes the system, making it universally legal to build.  
* **Weaknesses:** Bureaucratic standards processes take years, often decades, and rarely move without a standing, successful prototype to force the issue[38].  
* **Key Dependency:** Government willingness to amend EBCS EN 1996 to explicitly cover unreinforced earthen floor vaults.  
* **Probability:** **Very Low (as a first step).** Standards follow established practice; they rarely precede it.

## **Final Synthesis**

Based on the exhaustive analysis of the institutional pathways, historical analogues, and the local Ethiopian regulatory context, the core task questions are answered directly below:  
**What is the system’s current position on the journey from research to adoption?** The system is technically validated at the prototype scale (TRL 4/5), having definitively proven it can carry code-required static loads (2 kN/m²) using locally pressed CSEBs[4]. However, institutionally and implementation-wise, it is at TRL 1. It lacks regulatory recognition under the EBCS masonry codes, has never been priced or built by an independent Ethiopian commercial contractor, and lacks long-term post-occupancy durability data. (Epistemic status: Documented fact).  
**What type of institution is most likely to host the next meaningful project?** An academic or vocational institution, specifically **EiABC** or a regional TVET college. These institutions possess the legal mandate for innovation, land for experimentation, and the unique institutional cover to bypass standard municipal commercial permitting, which would currently reject an unreinforced earthen floor system outright[10].  
**What building type would generate the greatest combination of learning, credibility, and future opportunity?**  
A **single-story, permanent auxiliary structure** (such as a campus workshop, machinery storage facility, or rural classroom). This provides the technical learning of a real-world envelope (weather exposure, doors, functional use) and the visibility of a permanent structure, but keeps life-safety liability extremely low compared to a multi-story occupied residence. Crucially, it proves the system works *as a roof* that structurally mimics the exact behavior of a *floor* for a future second story.  
**What evidence would most increase the probability of a second project?** Not further academic load tests, but a **highly visual Contractor Construction Manual accompanied by a verified Cost/Time Audit**. The primary barrier to a second project will be execution risk. If Nebyu can prove that a standard masonry crew, using basic temporary guides, can build the vault accurately, quickly, and profitably—without requiring imported Catalan vault artisans or PhD supervision—contractors will pay attention. The SUDU project stalled precisely because the artisan execution was too complex[16].  
**Who is most likely to become a trust-carrying champion?** A **progressive NGO or Bilateral Donor** (e.g., Swiss Development Cooperation, UN-Habitat, UNIDO). Commercial engineers and contractors face too much personal and financial liability to champion a pre-code structural system. Donors, conversely, are actively incentivized to fund and champion high-risk, high-reward climate mitigation technologies that reduce embodied carbon[22].  
**What coalition is sufficient to support a first real building?**  
A tripartite, non-commercial coalition: **The Innovator** (Nebyu for technical control and geometry assurance) + **The Host** (EiABC for land and a regulatory sandbox) + **The Donor** (A Climate/Housing NGO for risk-free grant capital).  
**What pathway appears most plausible today?**  
The **University-Led Pathway** seamlessly transitioning into the **NGO-Led Pathway**. Nebyu should leverage his academic credentials to build a functional auxiliary structure on a university campus, funded by a micro-grant. He must document the *process of building it* meticulously. He then uses that standing building to convince an NGO to fund a rural clinic or school, proving the system can be executed by average rural contractors using locally pressed CSEB.  
**What assumptions must prove true for that pathway to work?**

1. That EiABC remains willing to host experimental earthen structures despite past logistical challenges[17].  
2. That the earthen barrel vault geometry can be executed by average Ethiopian masons using simple, repeatable guide-work, without requiring the extreme, unscalable precision of Catalan timbrel vaulting[16].  
3. That 3-7% cement-stabilized earth blocks (CSEBs) can be reliably sourced or pressed on-site to a consistent \>5 MPa standard using local soils[4].

**What could cause that pathway to stall?** The "Execution Friction." If the temporary falsework or geometric guides require more time, lumber, or skill to erect than the ubiquitous eucalyptus scaffolding currently used for ribbed HCB slabs[17], contractors will refuse to adopt it. Furthermore, if a seismic event in Addis Ababa (Zone 4\) highlights vulnerabilities in unreinforced earthen structures, institutional backing from universities and NGOs will evaporate instantly[5].  
**What should Nebyu do next if his goal is not adoption today, but progression toward eventual adoption?**  
He must stop optimizing the structural mathematics and start optimizing the construction logistics. His immediate next step is to initiate a conversation with an institutional host (EiABC) to secure a footprint to build a small, permanent, functional structure. During that build, his primary research output must be an open-source, highly visual **Construction Guide** that proves the system is not just structurally viable in a lab, but logistically viable on a dusty, resource-constrained Ethiopian job site. Only when the *process of building* is de-risked will the institutions follow.
