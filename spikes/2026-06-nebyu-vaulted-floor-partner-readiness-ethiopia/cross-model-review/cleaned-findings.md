## Purpose

This file distills the strongest findings from the two raw model generations and the cross-model comparison.

It is not a final partner-facing memo.

It is not a pitch.

It is a working synthesis to decide what should be prepared before partner outreach.

## Executive Finding

Nebyu’s vaulted earthen floor system appears to be past the “idea only” stage, but not yet at the “partner-ready building system” stage.

The public evidence supports a real research and testing trajectory: MIT thesis, conference/paper trail, a 3 m prototype, and a reported 15 m² full-scale slab test in Addis Ababa that MIT says withstood design loads. However, the public record reviewed through the model outputs does not yet show the artifacts institutions would need before hosting or approving a first monitored building: a detailed public test dossier, local engineer review, project-specific design package, contractor method statement, QA checklist, local BOQ, inspection logic, waterproofing plan, or post-occupancy monitoring precedent.

The next step should therefore be framed as:

> Partner discovery and evidence-pathway design.

Not:

> Adoption, commercialization, or a request to build occupied housing.

A partner discovery deck can be useful, but only if it is paired with a technical readiness memo and trust-gap matrix. A deck alone risks becoming premature persuasion.

## Cleaned Finding 1: The main problem is institutional trust, not only engineering merit

Both models agree that this is not simply a question of whether the geometry is clever or whether earthen masonry can work in compression.

For a floor system, institutions will ask:

- who signs the design
- who owns liability
- who inspects construction
- who verifies quality
- who supervises decentering or temporary supports
- who accepts occupancy risk
- who monitors performance
- what happens if cracks, settlement, moisture damage, or serviceability issues appear

A wall material can sometimes be trialed in a more contained way. A floor system carries more life-safety meaning.

### Working implication

Partner outreach should begin by asking what evidence would make a first monitored build reviewable, not by asking for adoption or deployment.

## Cleaned Finding 2: The system is more advanced than thesis-only, but partner-grade evidence remains incomplete

The raw generations differ on how far along Nebyu’s system is.

The ChatGPT generation identifies public evidence for:

- MIT thesis completion
- 2024 IASS paper
- 2025 SSRN preprint
- 3 m prototype
- MIT-reported 15 m² full-scale slab test in Addis Ababa

The Gemini generation emphasizes:

- MIT thesis
- modeling
- 3 m prototype
- CSEB production and material testing
- cost/carbon claims
- unresolved structural and construction unknowns

The best cleaned synthesis is:

> Public evidence indicates the system has advanced beyond thesis-only status, including a reported 15 m² full-scale slab test in Addis Ababa. However, a detailed public test dossier was not identified in the model outputs, so partner-grade technical readiness remains incomplete.

## Cleaned Finding 3: The 15 m² test is important, but it should not be overclaimed

The reported 15 m² full-scale Addis Ababa test is a major update. It likely changes the conversation from “academic concept” to “serious tested prototype.”

But it should not be used as proof that the system is ready for occupied buildings unless the detailed test evidence is available.

Needed details may include:

- drawings
- geometry
- material properties
- support and restraint details
- loading protocol
- instrumentation
- deflection and settlement data
- crack observations
- safety factor assumptions
- failure or non-failure criteria
- independent review
- photos / inspection notes
- lessons learned

### Working implication

One of Nebyu’s most important next artifacts is a concise, shareable technical dossier summarizing the 15 m² test.

## Cleaned Finding 4: The first partner ask should be a discovery question, not a build request

The models converge strongly here.

A first Habitat-style or NGO conversation should not ask:

- Will you build this in housing?
- Will you fund adoption?
- Will you use this in a school or clinic?
- Will you help us scale this?

It should ask:

- What evidence would your organization need before considering any monitored structural pilot?
- What kinds of buildings would be categorically impossible for a first test?
- What use types might be low enough risk to discuss?
- What local engineer or reviewer would you trust?
- What would your risk, legal, or safeguarding team reject immediately?
- What previous alternative-material pilots have failed institutionally, and why?

### Working implication

The goal of first outreach is not commitment. It is decision intelligence.

## Cleaned Finding 5: A partner discovery deck is useful but insufficient

Both models agree that a deck can open a conversation but cannot carry the institutional burden.

A deck may be appropriate for:

- NGO leadership
- donor program officers
- site-host discovery
- non-technical partner orientation

But it must not be the only artifact.

The minimum partner-readiness packet should include:

1. partner discovery deck or brief
2. technical readiness memo
3. trust-gap matrix
4. first-build concept note
5. list of partner discovery questions

For technical audiences, add:

6. engineer review packet
7. contractor constructability packet
8. QA / inspection draft
9. local BOQ or BOQ template

### Working implication

Nebyu can work on a deck, but the deck should be sober, short, and discovery-oriented. It should point to supporting packets rather than trying to persuade alone.

## Cleaned Finding 6: The first build should be evidence-generating, not symbolic

A demo that gets torn down, photographed, or treated as a one-off showcase may not help.

The first build should be designed as a gate:

> This build exists to produce the evidence required for the next decision.

The strongest staged pathway is:

1. technical dossier and local peer review
2. instrumented full-scale test bay / structural mock-up
3. restricted-use auxiliary building
4. monitored low-occupancy build
5. only later, classroom / housing / clinic use

### Strong first-build candidates

- instrumented test bay on controlled site
- restricted-use auxiliary building
- storage or workshop-type building with controlled occupancy
- university-hosted research structure
- non-beneficiary-facing pilot element

### Premature first-build candidates

- family housing
- school classroom
- clinic
- multi-story residential prototype
- highly visible public building
- anything requiring routine occupancy before the evidence package is stronger

## Cleaned Finding 7: The likely early blockers are non-technical as much as technical

The models agree that early failure could occur before a detailed engineering review even begins.

Likely blockers:

- no local engineer willing to review or sign
- no clear permitting category
- no site owner willing to host
- no liability assignment
- no contractor willing to price the work
- no QA or inspection checklist
- no waterproofing / maintenance plan
- no repeatable construction sequence
- no path from first build to second build
- no explicit monitoring responsibility

### Working implication

A technical readiness packet must be paired with an approval and responsibility map.

## Cleaned Finding 8: Contractor repeatability is a central trust gap

A system can work in a research build and still fail as a repeatable construction method.

The models identify several contractor-facing questions:

- Can ordinary crews build the vault accurately?
- How hard is the formwork?
- What tolerances matter?
- How is decentering controlled?
- How many skilled masons are required?
- How long does it take?
- What can go wrong on site?
- What inspections are needed before loading?
- Can the system be priced without large risk padding?

### Working implication

A contractor constructability packet should be created before any serious build ask.

## Cleaned Finding 9: Cost and carbon claims are useful but should not lead the conversation

The cost and carbon case matters, but it should not be used to bypass safety, liability, or review.

The safest framing is:

> The system may offer material, cost, and carbon advantages, but the next step is to determine what evidence would make a first monitored build responsible and reviewable.

Avoid leading with:

- “62% cheaper”
- “74% lower carbon”
- “replacement for concrete slabs”
- “ready for affordable housing”
- “scalable solution”

unless the evidence basis and assumptions are clearly stated.

### Working implication

Cost/carbon should be treated as potential value, not proof of readiness.

## Cleaned Finding 10: Language can either reduce or increase institutional risk perception

The models agree that language matters.

### Higher-risk wording

| Phrase | Why it may hurt |
|---|---|
| experimental system | triggers liability concern |
| pilot housing | sounds like end users are being used as test subjects |
| unreinforced floor | may alarm non-technical partners |
| earthen floor | may imply dirt floor or low-status material |
| low-cost replacement | may sound like cutting corners |
| ready to build | overclaims readiness |
| demonstration | may sound symbolic or disposable |

### Safer wording

| Safer phrase | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| monitored structural test | defines bounded learning |
| restricted-use research build | lowers occupancy concern |
| engineered compressed-earth masonry floor system | signals technical control |
| compression-based vaulted floor system | explains the structural logic |
| material-efficient structural system under review | avoids “cheap” framing |
| partner discovery and due diligence | lowers pressure |
| evidence-generating first build | avoids disposable demo framing |

## Cleaned Finding 11: Habitat-style outreach should be framed around learning constraints

For a Habitat-style partner, the right posture is not:

> Help us deploy this.

It is:

> Help us understand what your organization would need before this could ever be considered for a monitored, non-routine first build.

A first Habitat-style packet should include:

- one-page system overview
- current readiness-stage table
- known vs unknown matrix
- 15 m² test summary, if available
- proposed evidence-generating first-build options
- partner discovery questions
- explicit statement that occupied housing is not the immediate ask

Do not bring:

- mass rollout plan
- adoption pitch
- beneficiary-facing housing proposal
- unsupported claims
- polished sales deck without technical backup

## Cleaned Finding 12: The best next internal artifact is a trust-gap matrix

Before polishing the deck, TCUS and Nebyu should align on a matrix like:

| Claim | Current evidence | Missing evidence | Who cares | Decision blocked | Lowest-cost next action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

This prevents the deck from hiding uncertainty.

### Example rows

| Claim | Current evidence | Missing evidence | Who cares | Decision blocked | Lowest-cost next action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| System can carry design loads | Thesis / prototype / reported 15 m² test | public detailed test dossier | engineers, site hosts | technical review | summarize and share test data |
| System is cheaper | model-based estimate | contractor-priced local BOQ | NGOs, donors | funding / partner interest | QS + contractor pricing exercise |
| System can be built repeatably | prototype construction | method statement, training, QA checklist | contractors, EOR | first build | constructability workshop |
| System can survive wet conditions | general material logic | waterproofing and maintenance protocol | NGOs, site owner | site hosting | draft weathering plan |
| System has approval path | no public precedent found | local review and permit pre-consult | government, host | legal build | meet local engineer / permit advisor |

## Working Priority Order

### Must know before serious partner outreach

These should be clarified before asking a partner for anything beyond a discovery conversation:

1. What exactly was tested in the 15 m² full-scale slab?
2. Is there a shareable technical summary of that test?
3. What local engineer or faculty reviewer can review the current evidence?
4. What building use type is least risky but still useful?
5. What are the known unresolved structural, constructability, moisture, and inspection questions?
6. What is Nebyu not claiming yet?

### Useful but not required for first discovery conversations

These would strengthen outreach but should not block early learning conversations:

1. preliminary local BOQ
2. contractor constructability memo
3. first-build option table
4. language-risk map
5. partner-specific question list
6. comparison against ribbed/HCB baseline

### Downstream / later gate

These are important but likely premature before local peer review and a monitored first build:

1. formal code recognition
2. ministry-level adoption
3. commercial licensing model
4. school/classroom deployment
5. housing deployment
6. multi-story application
7. routine contractor package

## What Should Carry Into the Final Partner-Readiness Brief

The final brief should be organized around one practical question:

> What should Nebyu prepare before partner outreach, and what should he avoid asking for too early?

The final brief should include:

- current readiness stage
- evidence gaps
- decision blockers
- first-build options
- artifact recommendations
- language guidance
- next-action sequence

It should not include:

- broad history of construction innovation
- long technology promotion sections
- general climate case
- premature adoption roadmap
- unverified cost/carbon claims as conclusions

## Current Bottom Line

Nebyu should probably continue preparing a partner discovery deck, but the deck should be reframed.

It should not be a pitch deck.

It should be a discovery brief that says:

> This is what has been proven, this is what has not been proven, this is the kind of monitored evidence-generating first step we are exploring, and these are the institutional questions we need to answer before any responsible build.

The next institutional milestone is not adoption.

It is making the system reviewable.
