## Purpose

Compare the ChatGPT Deep Research and Gemini Pro Extended generations for the Nebyu vaulted floor partner-readiness spike.

This is not the cleaned findings file yet. It is a comparison layer that identifies where the two model outputs agree, where they differ, which claims look strongest, which claims are too confident, and what should be carried into the next spike artifacts.

The purpose of this spike is not to pitch Nebyu’s vaulted floor system. The purpose is to understand partner-readiness and institutional decision reality before outreach.

## High-Level Read

The two model generations converge on the same central finding:

> Nebyu’s vaulted floor system should not be treated as a material-substitution story. It is a construction-system and institutional-trust problem.

Both models agree that a partner discovery deck may be useful, but it is not sufficient by itself. Because the system intervenes in the floor structure, not just a wall, panel, block, or cladding material, any credible partner conversation needs to be paired with more decision-grade artifacts:

- a technical readiness memo
- a trust-gap matrix
- a first-build or monitored-test concept note
- a contractor constructability packet
- a clear list of what is known, unknown, and not yet ready to claim

The strongest shared conclusion is:

> The next institutional ask should not be “please adopt this” or even “please build this in housing.” It should be “help us define what evidence would make a first monitored, bounded, low-occupancy build reviewable.”

## Strongest Areas of Agreement

### 1. A pitch deck alone is insufficient

Both models agree that Nebyu should not rely on a single partner discovery deck.

A deck can open a conversation, especially with an NGO, donor, or Habitat-style partner. But it cannot answer the decision questions that matter for a structural floor system:

- who reviews the design
- who signs/stamps the design
- who owns construction-phase risk
- who owns post-occupancy risk
- how the build is inspected
- what use category is being proposed
- whether the next build is actually evidence-generating
- what path exists from first build to next gate

**Comparison read:** Strong. This should become a core cleaned finding.

### 2. The next conversation should be discovery and due diligence, not adoption

Both models agree that the current stage supports serious review, but not routine deployment.

The strongest safe framing is:

> “We are exploring what evidence and institutional conditions would make a first monitored, bounded pilot reviewable.”

Not:

> “We are ready to build housing with this system.”

**Comparison read:** Strong. This should guide the partner-facing language.

### 3. The system is more advanced than a thesis-only idea, but not yet a partner-ready building system

Both models treat the research status as meaningful and real.

Shared readiness baseline:

- concept and academic research are complete
- modeling is substantial
- a 3 m physical prototype is part of the record
- cost/carbon claims exist but remain assumption-sensitive
- a full partner-grade package is not yet public
- demonstration building / occupied-use precedent is not yet established in public evidence

ChatGPT adds an important public-status update: it found a 2025 MIT report of a 15 m² full-scale slab test in Addis Ababa that reportedly withstood all design loads. This should be treated as a major status update, but not as a substitute for a public detailed test dossier.

**Comparison read:** Strong, but source-clean the 15 m² claim and obtain the detailed report if possible.

### 4. The first real build should be low-occupancy, controlled, and evidence-generating

Both models reject jumping straight to a family home, school classroom, clinic, or multi-story residential use.

They converge on a staged path:

1. controlled full-scale test bay or structural mock-up
2. restricted-use auxiliary building or small non-primary structure
3. low-occupancy monitored building
4. only later, housing/classroom/clinic-type use

**Comparison read:** Strong. This should be central to partner-readiness advice.

### 5. The highest-risk bottleneck is not just engineering

Both outputs emphasize that technical performance is only one part of the challenge.

Likely early blockers include:

- no local engineer willing to sign or peer-review
- unclear permitting category
- site owner reluctance
- contractor uncertainty
- liability ambiguity
- missing inspection protocol
- missing QA checklist
- missing waterproofing / durability plan
- lack of a path from first build to second gate

**Comparison read:** Strong. This is the core value of the spike.

### 6. Floor-system innovation carries a higher trust burden than wall-system innovation

Both models distinguish Nebyu from Kubik, Agrostone, and CSEB walling.

A wall, partition, block, or cladding system can often be tested in a more contained way. A floor system affects life safety, live-load behavior, serviceability, support reactions, seismic response, inspection, and liability.

**Comparison read:** Strong. This should become one of the headline conclusions.

### 7. Language matters because “pilot” can trigger risk, not trust

Both models agree that partner-facing language should be carefully controlled.

Risky language:

- experimental system
- prototype building for families
- new floor system ready for adoption
- low-cost replacement for concrete slabs
- unreinforced earthen floor

Safer language, depending on audience:

- monitored structural test bay
- restricted-use research build
- engineered compressed-earth masonry floor system
- geometry-based structural efficiency
- bounded first-build study
- technical readiness review
- partner discovery and due-diligence conversation

**Comparison read:** Strong. This should be converted into a language-risk table.

## Where ChatGPT Deep Research Is Stronger

ChatGPT does a better job separating what public evidence supports, what is only partially supported, and what no public evidence was found for. It flags the difference between a public MIT article reporting a 15 m² full-scale test and the absence of a detailed public test dossier.

**Keep.** This should become the evidence standard for the cleaned findings.

### 2. Institutional decision sequencing

ChatGPT’s strongest contribution is sequencing:

- first, establish reality baseline
- then identify decision blockers
- then choose first-build option
- then decide artifacts
- then conduct partner conversations

This avoids letting the deck become the process.

**Keep.** This should become the partner-readiness logic.

### 3. Addis Ababa / Ethiopian permitting logic

ChatGPT attempts to anchor the institutional analysis in Ethiopian permitting and risk-level logic rather than generic bureaucracy. It emphasizes that a first monitored building is not merely a design choice; it sits inside planning consent, consultant engagement, supervision, inspections, and occupancy certification.

**Keep, but source-clean.** Any specific claim about permit categories, risk matrices, consultant requirements, or inspection sequence should be verified before being presented externally.

### 4. Better first-building caution

ChatGPT is very useful on the first-building question. It argues that the first serious build is probably not:

- a house for end users
- a classroom
- a clinic
- a flashy pavilion

Instead, it recommends a controlled, monitored, instrumented structure that produces evidence for the next gate.

**Keep.** This is one of the most actionable outputs.

### 5. Better artifact taxonomy

ChatGPT gives a practical set of artifacts:

- short partner discovery brief
- technical readiness memo
- trust-gap matrix
- engineer review packet
- contractor constructability packet
- monitored pilot concept note
- permitting pre-consult note

**Keep.** This should feed directly into `partner-readiness-brief.md`.

## Main Tensions Between the Models

### Tension 1: How far along is the technical system?

ChatGPT says public evidence now supports a more advanced baseline than thesis-only because of a reported 15 m² full-scale floor slab test in Addis Ababa.

Gemini focuses mainly on the thesis and 3 m prototype, and treats physical prototyping as partial.

**Resolution:**  
The cleaned findings should say:

> Public evidence indicates the system has advanced beyond thesis-only status, including a reported 15 m² full-scale slab test. However, no detailed public test dossier was found in this pass, so partner-grade technical readiness remains incomplete.

### Tension 2: How confident can we be about institutional behavior?

ChatGPT is more cautious and labels many institutional conclusions as inference.

Gemini is more declarative, sometimes stating that NGOs, permit officials, engineers, or insurers “will” refuse under specific conditions.

**Resolution:**  
Use Gemini’s institutional refusal patterns as hypotheses, not facts. Phrase as:

> likely concern  
> plausible refusal point  
> needs local review  
> should be tested in discovery conversations

### Tension 3: What is the first build?

ChatGPT favors an instrumented test bay or controlled structural build, then a restricted-use auxiliary building.

Gemini also favors a test bay and monitored auxiliary build, but is more explicit about a multi-step gate sequence.

**Resolution:**  
The models agree enough to produce a staged recommendation:

1. technical dossier and local peer review
2. instrumented full-scale test bay / structural mock-up
3. restricted-use auxiliary building
4. monitored low-occupancy build
5. only later, classroom/housing/clinic

### Tension 4: What should Nebyu bring to Habitat?

ChatGPT says a sober discovery packet, technical readiness memo, trust-gap matrix, and monitored-pilot concept note.

Gemini says bring a discovery deck, prototype photos, and monitored pilot concept note, while avoiding a family-home proposal or commercial rollout plan.

**Resolution:**  
For a first Habitat-style conversation, Nebyu should bring:

- a short discovery deck/brief
- a one-page readiness-stage summary
- a trust-gap matrix
- a proposed evidence-generating first-build concept
- a list of questions for Habitat

He should not bring:

- an adoption pitch
- a request to build occupied housing immediately
- a commercial product brochure
- unsupported cost/carbon claims
- language implying the system is already partner-ready

### Tension 5: How much should cost/carbon claims lead?

Both models mention cost and carbon reductions, but both also warn that these are not enough.

**Resolution:**  
Cost/carbon should be secondary in partner outreach. The lead should be:

> “We are exploring a bounded, monitored, evidence-generating pathway.”

Cost and carbon claims can be presented as potential value, not as the reason to bypass safety and institutional due diligence.

## Proposed Partner-Readiness Logic

The next stage should not be described as “commercialization” or “adoption.”

It should be described as:

> Partner discovery and evidence-pathway design.

The sequence should be:

1. **Internal readiness packet**
   - what exists
   - what is unknown
   - what is not being claimed

2. **Technical peer-review conversation**
   - local engineer / EiABC / structural reviewer
   - ask what would make the system reviewable

3. **Habitat-style partner discovery**
   - ask what evidence would be needed before they could even consider engagement
   - do not ask them to host occupied housing yet

4. **First-build concept selection**
   - test bay or restricted-use auxiliary building
   - designed to answer specific evidence gaps

5. **Constructability and QA package**
   - method statement
   - inspection checklist
   - tolerances
   - waterproofing and maintenance logic

6. **Monitored build proposal**
   - only after technical and partner reality checks

## Artifact Recommendation

### Artifact 1: Partner Discovery Brief / Deck

**Audience:** Habitat-style NGO, donor, institutional partner, potential site host  
**Purpose:** Open a serious due-diligence conversation  
**Should include:**

- problem context
- what the system is
- current readiness stage
- what has been tested
- what is unknown
- why the next step is monitored learning, not adoption
- questions for the partner

**Should not include:**

- adoption pitch
- sweeping cost/carbon claims
- “ready to scale” language
- request to build occupied housing immediately

### Artifact 2: Technical Readiness Memo

**Audience:** local structural engineer, EiABC/AAU faculty, technical reviewer  
**Purpose:** Make the system reviewable  
**Should include:**

- geometry and typologies
- load testing summary
- support/thrust logic
- material specs
- prototype and full-scale-test status
- unresolved structural questions
- seismic and serviceability unknowns
- list of documents available or missing

### Artifact 3: Trust-Gap Matrix

**Audience:** TCUS, Nebyu, Robel, technical reviewers, serious partners  
**Purpose:** Keep everyone honest about what must be learned before a first build  
**Should include:**

- claim
- existing evidence
- missing evidence
- who needs to be convinced
- how to reduce uncertainty
- decision unlocked

### Artifact 4: First-Build Concept Note

**Audience:** university, donor, NGO, controlled site host  
**Purpose:** Define a first build as an evidence-generating gate  
**Should include:**

- building option
- use restrictions
- monitoring plan
- inspection plan
- stop criteria
- learning objectives
- responsibility map

### Artifact 5: Contractor Constructability Packet

**Audience:** contractor, foreman, QS, site supervisor  
**Purpose:** Test whether the system can be priced, sequenced, and built by ordinary crews  
**Should include:**

- construction sequence
- formwork/decentering plan
- tolerances
- block requirements
- QA checkpoints
- labor assumptions
- BOQ template
