## **Phase 1 — Reality Baseline: What Is Actually Known?**

To effectively approach institutional partners, the current state of the vaulted earthen floor system must be established utilizing the best available empirical evidence, stripping away academic optimism and focusing strictly on verifiable technical reality. The working premise positions this system not merely as a material substitution, but as a fundamental construction-system innovation that replaces conventional reinforced concrete floor slabs with an unreinforced, compression-based geometry utilizing earthen masonry. Because failure of a floor system impacts life safety, professional liability, and occupancy permitting, the threshold for institutional trust is exceptionally high.

The foundational evidence for this system is primarily documented in a 2024 Master of Science thesis at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, titled "Low-Cost Masonry for the Design of Barrel-Vaulted Flooring Systems," authored by Nebyu Samuel Haile and supervised by John A. Ochsendorf.[1] The academic research phase is complete, providing a robust theoretical framework. Structural modeling has been extensively executed, incorporating 2D equilibrium analysis on single tributary bays, parametric modeling at 0.01-meter intervals for span-to-rise ratios, specifically evaluating span/10 and span/20, and 3D Thrust Network Optimization (TNO) to corroborate the distribution of forces.[3] These models confirm the theoretical stability of the "Sparse Ribs," "Dense Ribs," and "Light Fill" floor typologies under International Building Code (IBC) loading parameters.

Physical prototyping has been partially completed. A full-scale 3-meter span prototype featuring a 1.5-meter depth, a 5 cm thickness, and a highly shallow 1/20 span-to-rise ratio was constructed in a controlled laboratory environment.[3] This prototype underwent static load testing, subjected to unfactored IBC residential live loads of 2 kN/m² applied both symmetrically and asymmetrically.[1] Symmetrical loading generated working stresses of 0.5 MPa, well below the compressive capacity of the blocks, while asymmetrical loading confirmed that the thrust line remained within the primary vault geometry, satisfying Heyman’s Safe Theorem.[3] However, the physical load test revealed a critical vulnerability: the thesis notes a documented fact that a "3.8 cm vertical deflection was observed at the center of the vault," which was "structurally linked to a minor 6.6 mm horizontal settlement at the supports."[1] This horizontal displacement occurred because the supporting steel angles were tied but not bolted down, allowing rotation.[1] For a compression-only structure, any horizontal displacement at the supports threatens arch action, making this 6.6 mm settlement a major technical hurdle for future engineering reviews.

Field construction and materials testing in Ethiopia have been executed, though limited to component-level block production rather than full-scale floor system assembly. During field testing supported by the Ethiopian Institute of Architecture, Building Construction and City Development (EiABC), Compressed Stabilized Earth Bricks (CSEBs) were produced using local soil.[1] Due to equipment variations, bricks in Ethiopia were compacted using a hydraulic press at a pressure of 4 MPa, requiring a custom steel "lip" to overfill the molds and allow single-pass compaction.[1] The material matrix utilized 3% and 7% cement stabilization, bonded with a 1:10:4 (cement-sand-soil) mud mortar.[1]

The claims being made regarding the system's performance are highly specific but represent technical inferences based on analytical models rather than empirical post-occupancy data. The system claims up to a 62% reduction in cost, citing a decrease from $153/m² to $58/m² for the "Dense Ribs" typology.[3] Carbon reduction claims assert a 74% decrease in embodied carbon, dropping from 72 kgCO₂e/m² to 19 kgCO₂e/m², alongside a 57% reduction in structural weight.[3] Constructability claims focus on the significant reduction of imported cement and steel, which currently account for up to 90% of residential construction expenses in Less Economically Developed Countries (LEDCs).[1] The comparison baseline utilized to generate these cost and carbon claims is a 17 cm thick C35 concrete two-way flat slab containing approximately 0.7% steel reinforcement, modeled within a theoretical 15-story Ethiopian building context.[3]

Despite these verified models and prototype tests, significant unknowns remain. The system lacks empirical data regarding long-term creep under sustained dead loads, dynamic seismic response in Ethiopia's geologically active regions, acoustic separation performance for multi-family dwellings, fire resistance ratings, and integration methodologies for mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) systems. Crucially, the vulnerability of the earthen masonry to moisture ingress and structural degradation over subsequent Ethiopian rainy seasons remains unquantified at a systems level.

## **Phase 2 — Institutional Friction Map: What Blocks Engagement?**

Institutional actors do not evaluate structural innovations purely on environmental merit or theoretical cost savings; they evaluate them through the restrictive lenses of liability, regulatory compliance, and operational friction. Mapping how different institutions will interpret the vaulted floor system reveals significant non-technical blockers that must be addressed before persuasion can begin.

University and engineering faculties, such as those at the Ethiopian Institute of Architecture, Building Construction and City Development (EiABC), will likely classify this system as an experimental academic research project. They are primarily interested in publishing opportunities, the testing of local materials, and the pedagogical value of student training, much like their previous engagement with the Sustainable Urban Dwelling Unit (SUDU) project.[6] However, their engagement will be blocked by a lack of dedicated funding and the strict refusal to accept life-safety liability if the system is proposed for an occupied campus building. The evidence they inherently trust consists of laboratory test reports, peer-reviewed academic papers, and finite element models. Consequently, a university could make the decision today to host a non-occupied test bay on campus, but they cannot and will not approve the system for a multi-story university dormitory.

Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and Habitat-style housing organizations will view the system as a high-risk, unproven material substitution. Their interest is heavily anchored in the claims of a 62% cost savings, a 74% carbon reduction, and the potential for local job creation through decentralized CSEB production.[3] Conversely, they will pause or refuse engagement due to fears of structural liability, the requirement for specialized masonry labor, and long-term maintenance uncertainty. NGOs harbor institutional trauma from previous alternative building material failures in Ethiopia, and they require evidence in the form of post-occupancy evaluations, government approvals, and 1:1 scale physical mockups before committing. At present, an NGO might decide to fund a minor feasibility study or a small non-residential auxiliary building, but they cannot yet substitute their standard reinforced concrete housing models with the vaulted system.

Donors and philanthropic foundations will classify the vaulted floor as a climate-tech and green industrialization innovation. They are intensely interested in decarbonization metrics, circular economy alignment, and the potential for scalability across Sub-Saharan Africa.[8] Their primary hesitation stems from the high risk of project abandonment and the historic lack of local government buy-in for non-standard construction methods. Donors rely on comprehensive Lifecycle Assessments (LCAs), successful pilot metrics, and alignment with national policies, such as Ethiopia's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC).[8] A donor could currently make the decision to provide grant funding for a deeply monitored pilot, but they cannot scale that funding to a national housing program until systemic viability is proven.

Government housing bodies will interpret the earthen vaulted floor as a non-standard, non-code-compliant anomaly. While they are conceptually interested in solutions that alleviate chronic cement and steel shortages and localize supply chains,[10] their operational mandate forces them to reject deviations from established codes. The institutional memory of the "Agrostone" alternative material rollout, which suffered from severe moisture absorption and durability issues,[12] makes government bodies highly skeptical of waste-based or earthen materials. They require stamped approvals from recognized local engineering authorities and adherence to Ethiopian Standards Agency (ESA) parameters. A government housing body might currently allow a restricted pilot within a highly controlled regulatory sandbox, but they absolutely cannot adopt the system into the mass-scale Integrated Housing Development Programme (IHDP).

Municipal permitting bodies, operating at the Wereda or City level, will view the system strictly as a category anomaly under the Ethiopian Building Proclamation 624/2009. The Proclamation clearly delineates buildings into Categories A, B, and C, heavily predicated on standard spans between reinforced concrete or steel structural frames.[5] Permitting officials have no inherent interest in the system's carbon metrics; their interest lies solely in maintaining standard checklist reviews. They will actively resist the system because they lack the prescriptive code provisions or software to verify the structural integrity of an unreinforced vault.[14] The only evidence a permitting body trusts is a wet-ink stamp from an Ethiopian licensed Category 1 Structural Engineer. Therefore, their immediate decision would be to reject the application or demand an exhaustive external peer review, meaning they cannot issue a standard building permit for a multi-story structure under current conditions.

Structural engineers and Engineers of Record (EOR) will classify the system as a high-liability compression structure. While they may possess intellectual curiosity regarding the unique geometry and Thrust Network Optimization,[4] they will refuse engagement upon learning of the 6.6 mm horizontal support settlement observed in the MIT prototype.[1] Because they carry ultimate legal liability for structural failure, the lack of seismic behavior data and the reliance on unreinforced earthen spans will force them to pause. They require comprehensive design guidelines, peer-reviewed engineering manuals, and physical load tests demonstrating a high factor of safety, for example 3.0 or greater.[3] An EOR could currently advise on tie-rod detailing and boundary condition mechanics, but they cannot and will not stamp a multi-story commercial or residential design.

Contractors will view the system as presenting complex, labor-intensive formwork risks. They might be interested if the material costs are truly 60% cheaper, offering potential for higher profit margins. However, contractors will balk at the unknown timeline for formwork stripping (decentering), the complex masonry requirements, and the curing times of mud mortars. They trust step-by-step method statements, strictly priced Bills of Quantities (BOQs), and guaranteed payment terms regardless of the test outcome. A contractor could currently price a localized test bay by padding it with high-risk financial contingencies, but they cannot offer a fixed-price lump-sum contract for a multi-story build.

Site owners will classify the vault as a novelty structure. They are interested in lower capital expenditure and unique architectural aesthetics, but their primary fears include water leaks, structural cracking, and the resulting inability to insure or sell the asset. They require precedent buildings that have survived multiple Ethiopian rainy seasons without degradation. A site owner might provide a small plot of land for a temporary or auxiliary structure today, but they cannot commission a primary residence or commercial asset using this technology.

Insurers and liability holders will view the floor as an uninsurable experimental prototype. They have no interest in the system's efficiency; they strictly prefer standard actuarial risks. The complete lack of long-term performance data, fire ratings, or acoustic separation data will result in immediate refusal. Insurers require decades of actuarial data or strict government certification to provide coverage. They will currently decline coverage for the structural system, meaning they cannot underwrite a multi-family residential building.

Several non-technical blockers severely compound this friction. The foremost blocker is that no Ethiopian engineer will willingly stamp the design. The reliance on horizontal thrust resolution via steel ties dictates that any minor settlement can induce hinge formation and subsequent collapse; local engineers, constrained by strict liability and limited to standard reinforced concrete analysis software, will refuse to assume this risk without comprehensive financial and legal indemnity. Furthermore, the unclear permitting category under Proclamation 624/2009 ensures a bureaucratic freeze at the Wereda level.[5] Even if permitted, there is no existing inspection protocol; municipal inspectors routinely verify rebar spacing and concrete cover, but they possess no checklist to evaluate the compaction density of a CSEB vault or the tensioning of a steel tie-rod during the critical decentering phase.[14] Finally, the procurement mismatch regarding specialized block production and the lack of a post-occupancy monitoring plan create a scenario where there is no clear path from a successful first build to a scalable second build.

## **Phase 3 — First Building Reality Test**

To generate the specific evidence required to overcome the institutional friction mapped above, a physical building must be constructed. However, an undefined "pilot" or generic "demo" is useless unless it systematically retires targeted risks. The next step cannot be assumed to be a commercial product, nor should it introduce life-safety liabilities prematurely.

A test bay represents the most viable immediate option. Operating with very low safety and liability risk, a 1:1 scale structural bay constructed on a university campus or private testing ground faces very low permitting friction because it requires no certificate of occupancy. Its monitoring value is exceptionally high, as it can be loaded to failure without endangering lives, and its learning value is equally high regarding the logistics of formwork erection and decentering. While it lacks public visibility and similarity to future continuous-use scenarios, it poses a high risk of becoming a dead-end demo if it only proves the structure works theoretically without proving its utility. Nonetheless, it serves as an excellent Gate 1 intervention.

A pavilion carries low safety and liability risk and low permitting friction, but it offers poor monitoring value because it lacks floor loading. While it teaches intermediate lessons regarding geometry and masonry, it has high public visibility. However, because a roof is fundamentally different from an intermediate floor slab carrying active live loads, a pavilion bears a high risk of being a dead-end demo that fails to prove the system's core innovation. It is an overall poor fit.

A single-room structure offers low liability risk and medium permitting friction. Its monitoring value is high regarding thermal performance and moisture resilience, and its learning value is robust concerning the critical wall-to-vault connections. With medium public visibility and medium similarity to future use, it represents an excellent Gate 2 target. Similarly, a storage or auxiliary building operates under low safety risk and low permitting friction, likely qualifying as a Category A minor structure. It provides medium monitoring value and high learning value, making it another excellent Gate 2 option to test long-term durability.

Structures intended for human assembly, such as classrooms, community facilities, or clinic support buildings, introduce unacceptably high safety and liability risks. They face extreme permitting friction regarding public safety and critical infrastructure codes. While their learning and monitoring values are high, introducing dynamic human loads before establishing basic moisture and creep data invites bureaucratic rejection and catastrophic legal risk. These options are entirely premature. Workshops present medium liability risks and medium permitting friction, offering high learning value regarding vibration and impact loads, but they remain premature for a first intervention.

Small housing prototypes introduce medium safety risks but high permitting friction due to strict habitability codes. While they provide excellent monitoring value for acoustic separation and MEP integration, moving directly to a housing prototype without first retiring structural and moisture risks is a Gate 3 target, not a Gate 1 starting point. Multi-bay demonstration buildings and two-story prototypes carry high safety risks, very high permitting friction, requiring Category B or C approvals, and demand complex continuous thrust resolution across multiple bays. Attempting these builds immediately is dangerously premature.

The recommended staged sequence begins with a Monitored Test Bay (Non-Occupied). This is the lowest-risk build that still teaches something fundamentally important. By constructing a 1:1 scale structural bay utilizing local CSEBs and local labor on a secure site, the project retires the primary engineering risk. It allows an Engineer of Record to physically witness the erection, decentering, and settlement behavior without assuming life-safety liability. This build generates the critical evidence needed for the next gate: constructability time studies, true localized cost data via an actual Bill of Quantities, and strain/settlement data under sustained loads.

Following the success of the test bay, the sequence should advance to a Single-Room Auxiliary Building, such as a secure storage facility or guard house. In this structure, the vaulted system acts as the roof/ceiling but is artificially loaded with sandbags or water tanks to simulate a second-story live load. This build is essential to test the system's vulnerability to Ethiopia's torrential rainy seasons. As demonstrated by the historical failure of Agrostone panels, water absorption is a fatal flaw for alternative materials in Ethiopia.[12] This auxiliary build generates vital evidence regarding moisture resilience, waterproofing protocols, and establishes a Category A permitting precedent.

Attempting a multi-story residential prototype or a classroom at this stage would be far too risky and premature. Conversely, constructing an open-air pavilion might look architecturally impressive, but it would fail to create momentum because it does not prove the vault can function as an intermediate floor slab carrying live loads, resolving acoustic transmission, or integrating MEP services.

## **Phase 6 — Language Risk Check**

Terminology that signals success, funding readiness, or innovation in an academic or venture capital setting frequently signals liability, danger, or amateurism in an institutional construction setting.

The term "innovation" helps on grant applications but hurts severely during permitting and engineering reviews, where Wereda officials and EORs view deviation from the norm as a legal risk. A safer alternative is "material optimization" or "geometry-based structural efficiency." "Prototype" is useful in university labs but triggers fear for NGO risk managers and site owners; it should be replaced with "1:1 Structural Test Bay." Similarly, "pilot" helps secure donor funding but blocks occupancy permitting with housing ministries, making "Monitored Auxiliary Build" a safer phrase.

The phrase "experimental system" should never be used externally, as it guarantees rejection by insurers, EORs, and permitting officials. It must be reframed as a "Validated Structural Geometry." Discussing a "vaulted floor" is useful in architectural design discussions but hurts when engaging contractors or end-users who may falsely infer a loss of flat usable space on the floor above; "compression-based flat-topped slab" clarifies the functional reality.

"Earthen floor" is excellent for carbon footprint discussions but highly detrimental when presenting to NGOs or homeowners, as it implies a regression to primitive dirt floors. "Engineered Compressed Earth Masonry (CSEB)" establishes industrial credibility. The term "alternative floor system" is useful for policy discussions but instantly triggers the institutional trauma associated with Agrostone's failures for Ethiopian contractors and engineers.[12] It is safer to position it as a "low-carbon concrete-masonry hybrid." "Low-cost system" helps with NGO targeting but hurts when end-users or EORs interpret "low-cost" as "cheap and unsafe." It should be elevated to a "high-efficiency material system." Finally, "non-standard construction" and "monitored research build" should be replaced with "optimized masonry assembly" to avoid alarming insurers and regulatory bodies.

## **Phase 7 — Comparative Lessons Only Where Useful**

Understanding the Ethiopian construction technology landscape provides a vital roadmap for avoiding previous systemic failures and mimicking successful adoption strategies.

The adoption of the ribbed/HCB (Hollow Concrete Block) slab provides a baseline for integration.[11] Trust was carried by government standard setters and imported prefabrication systems that adapted to extreme material scarcity (cement and steel). Scale was not blocked because the system still relied fundamentally on reinforced concrete, meaning engineering liability and calculation methods remained standard. The lesson for Nebyu is that deep standardization and alignment with existing engineering software is the only path to national scale. However, the comparison is limited because ribbed slabs do not radically alter the tensile mechanics of the floor.

Agrostone, heavily promoted by the Addis Ababa Housing Development Project Office, serves as a cautionary tale.[10] Initially championed for a 50% cost savings on partition walls, the material suffered severe reputational and physical damage due to high water absorption, up to 22%, poor durability in wet conditions, and a subsequent total loss of structural trust.[12] Because earthen and waste-based materials carry this stigma in Ethiopia, the vaulted system will be heavily scrutinized for moisture failure. The limit of this comparison is that Agrostone was non-load bearing; the vaulted system carries exponentially higher life-safety risks.

Kubik, a startup transforming plastic waste into building materials, successfully navigated Ethiopian bureaucracy by carrying trust through third-party European standard certification (Intertek), verifying strength, fire resistance, and non-toxicity.[20] The lesson for Nebyu is to seek third-party international certification early to bypass local skepticism. However, Kubik builds vertical walls; intermediate floors have exponentially higher liability thresholds.

The Sustainable Urban Dwelling Unit (SUDU) project, executed by ETH Zurich and the EiABC, is the most direct analogue. It successfully constructed a double-story building in Addis Ababa utilizing rammed earth and Guastavino tile vaults without steel or concrete.[6] Trust was carried entirely by the prestige of the academic institutions. However, scaling was blocked by an over-reliance on local eucalyptus trees for formwork scaffolding and a lack of local maintenance knowledge.[6] The critical lesson is that the logistics of formwork and local skill transfer are just as critical as the mathematical viability of the vault. The comparison is limited because SUDU remained a bespoke academic showcase, not a commercialized floor system.

Limestone Calcined Clay Cement (LC3) provides a lesson in policy alignment. Trust is carried by the Ministry of Industry, ESA, and the Climate Technology Centre and Network (CTCN).[9] LC3 succeeded by presenting lab data proving equivalence to Portland cement and explicitly aligning with Ethiopia's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC) for decarbonization.[9] Nebyu must similarly align his carbon reduction claims with national Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) goals. However, LC3 is a 1:1 material swap that does not disrupt the construction workflow, whereas the vaulted floor requires a complete systemic overhaul.
