## Reality baseline

### What is directly supported

Nebyu’s 2024 MIT thesis is publicly indexed as **“Low-Cost Masonry for the Design of Barrel-Vaulted Flooring Systems,”** authored by Nebyu Samuel Haile with John A. Ochsendorf as advisor. The 2024 IASS paper and 2025 SSRN preprint show that the research agenda is an **unreinforced barrel-vaulted compression-only masonry floor system** intended as an alternative to conventional slab construction in low-cost housing contexts. [2]

The 2024 public technical paper is not just conceptual. It reports a **3-meter span physical prototype**, static analysis, parametric span studies, and a cost/carbon comparison against a **concrete flat slab** from a recently completed **15-story building in Addis Ababa**. In that paper, the prototype is described as 3 m span, 1.5 m deep, 5 cm thick, built with **3% cement-stabilized CSEB of 11 MPa strength**, resting on **steel angles** and connected by **steel ties at each stiffening wall** to manage thrust. That is a crucial nuance for partner outreach: the public record supports a system that avoids conventional slab reinforcement, but it does **not** support a claim that the tested system is “zero steel” or free of edge restraint hardware. [3]

The strongest public status update comes from MIT’s August 2025 article, which says Nebyu Haile “completed the design, construction, and full-scale testing of a **15 m² unreinforced barrel-vaulted floor slab system in Addis Ababa, Ethiopia**” and that the prototype “**successfully withstood all design loads**.” The same source identifies an ongoing collaboration involving **MIT-Africa**, **John Ochsendorf**, and **Assistant Professor Asgedom Haile of EiABC**. [4]

### What is only partially supported

The public technical case for savings is real, but it is still partly model-based and assumption-sensitive. The 2024 IASS paper reports **up to 74% lower embodied carbon** and **up to 62% lower cost** than a typical concrete flat slab, but it also states that material and labor costs were taken from **interviews with local engineers** and CSEB test data in Ethiopia; those figures therefore sit above pure marketing claims but below the confidence level of a contractor-priced procurement package or audited project BOQ. [5]

The public materials support structural feasibility under the load cases tested, but they do **not** close the trust gap on seismic behavior, construction tolerance sensitivity, serviceability at building scale, or integration into a repeatable building delivery process. The 2024 paper explicitly says the analysis is confined to static behavior, recommends safety factors because of CSEB variability, notes that **shallower span-to-rise ratios leave little room for construction error**, and says future research is needed on **seismic safety**, construction process, and **building-scale** behavior. [5]

A second important partial support point is the comparison baseline. In the public technical record reviewed here, the explicit baseline is a **concrete flat slab**, not a local Ethiopian **ribbed/HCB** or beam-and-block slab baseline. That means Nebyu’s cost and carbon case is publicly legible, but it is not yet publicly framed in the exact comparison language many Ethiopian engineers or housing actors may actually use. [6]

No public evidence found in this pass for a public **engineer-of-record stamped design**, a public **permit precedent**, a public **demonstration building in occupied service**, a public **engineering design guide**, a public **contractor method statement**, a public **QA checklist**, or a public **post-occupancy monitoring report** for Nebyu’s system. ## Institutional friction map

The public Ethiopian permitting environment points to a simple but important reality: a first monitored building is not just a technical decision. It sits inside a system of **planning consent**, consultant engagement, risk categorization, supervision duties, inspections, and occupancy certification. The Addis Ababa risk-level directive explicitly ties project category to supervision and inspection requirements, while the Ethiopian Business Portal says permit applicants must submit an agreement with a **design consultant/company**. A recent ASTU thesis abstract also says that structural, electrical, and sanitary drawings are reviewed by separate corresponding experts. That does not prove the exact “stamping” workflow for a novel floor system, but it strongly suggests that a serious partner will ask very early: **Which licensed consultant is willing to own the structural design?** [11]

The same Ethiopian framework suggests that not all first-build options are equal. Addis Ababa’s risk-level matrix shows that low-risk one-story buildings with low occupancy can move with much lighter supervision, while higher-risk buildings require an external firm or registered consulting company, geotechnical documentation, and city inspections at every stage of construction. That makes “small, low-occupancy, tightly controlled, evidence-generating” a much more plausible first step than a family house, school classroom, or public-use building. [12]

> **Note:** Addis Ababa Building Risk Level Matrix from the city’s 2020 directive. Lower-risk buildings trigger lighter supervision and inspection requirements; higher-risk buildings require external supervision and more frequent inspections.

## First-building reality test

The public code and permit evidence points toward a narrow but important conclusion: the first serious institutionally plausible build is probably **not** a house for end-users and probably **not** a classroom or clinic. Buildings with more people, public use, or more than one story quickly move into categories that require external supervision, geotechnical studies, and more intensive inspections. A first monitored building should therefore minimize occupancy and duty-of-care exposure **while still producing the missing evidence that matters for the next gate**. [12]

The strongest evidence-generating move is to treat the next step as a **controlled research building or monitored demonstration element**, not a market pilot. This fits both the current status of Nebyu’s work and the way historical Ethiopian technology pathways were documented: the GIZ low-cost housing manual paired new technologies with manuals, training, built examples, and project-specific structural engineering rather than pure product marketing. [21]

## Useful comparative lessons and priority-ordered next actions

The most useful historical Ethiopian analogue remains the earlier ribbed/HCB pathway, not because the systems are identical, but because both are **floor-system questions**. The GIZ low-cost housing manual shows that Ethiopian low-cost construction pathways did not normalize through an abstract idea alone. They used **manuals, standardized elements, training, built examples, and project-specific structural design**. One especially relevant sentence in that manual is that the system guide “is not meant to be a substitute for a structural design made by a professional structural engineer” and that structural calculation “has to be done for every project separately.” That is probably the single best historical caution for Nebyu’s current stage. [19]

CSEB and local-material wall systems are also useful, but only up to a point. Habitat Ethiopia’s public materials show actual housing projects using **stabilized soil blocks**, HCB, and fire bricks. That proves that local-material experimentation is not automatically outside the Ethiopian housing field. But wall systems are not floor systems, and Habitat’s public portfolio does not show first-of-kind structural floor pilots. Nebyu can borrow the **local-material**, **monitoring**, and **partnered implementation** logic, but not assume the same trust threshold. [14]

International vault analogues reinforce the same lesson. The **Nubian Vault Association** emphasizes trained and certified masons and reports buildings across homes, schools, and health centers. The **Auroville Earth Institute** combines vault R&D with manuals, design courses, and codified training materials; it also highlights government approval in some of its block systems. By contrast, the ETH/HiLo work shows that even a spectacular full-scale demonstration can remain a research showcase rather than a normalized delivery pathway. For Nebyu, the lesson is that geometry and full-scale testing matter, but **training, manuals, repeated execution, and institutional trust-carriers** matter just as much. [33]
